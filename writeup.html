Introduction

Disregarding the 2020 pandemic, more people today are flying than ever, and it is one of the primary sources of domestic travel. This creates a problem that almost all of us have experienced: packed airports. Whether you are at JFK or LAX or even General Mitchel International (MKE), sometimes it is a drag to get on your flight. However less packed flight are less efficient and are thus harmful to the environment. And there is evidence that in recent times there are increasing numbers of low passenger count flight. Using flight data from 2009 and GDP statistics from the year 2017, we can examine cities and their flight patterns. More importantly with this data we can determine if there is a link between the GDP of a city and the number and fullness of flights to that city. 1

Looking at our plot the passenger density is much higher when the GDP of cities are higher. The more densely packed the plane the better the plane trip is on our environment. Notably most of the passenger counts are clustered in the smaller cities with lower GDPs. We can see that the larger cities such as New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, have much higher passenger amounts. Interpretation: When graphing GDP vs.Passenger Density at a glance it can be seen that density does rise when GDP is higher.

Discussion

It can be concluded that the higher the GDP of a city, the more flights there will be to that city and the fuller those flights will be. On a broader level, this indicates that an economically stronger city leads to more people going to that location. This interpretation is based on the trends in the graphs and regression above that support this trend.

Potential issues inherent to our analysis include the mismatch in years between the flight year and GDP year, as the flights we focused on, are from 2009 while the GDP data of those cities is from 2017. This mismatch, depending on how much the citys GDP changed, could cause certain trends or patterns to be missed. However, this likely wont lead to too many problems given wealthy cities should still be wealthy a decade later barring larger economic issues. An additional issue is that our focus on a single year could lead to misunderstandings since it disallows us from seeing larger trends and detecting outlying data points. After all, there have been a lot of events in the last few decades that have affected travel trends. Namely, the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic and the 2008 housing crisis and economic recession.

To conclude our in-depth data analysis, we have found that cities with more flights (both inbound and outbound) and higher-density flights in 2009 generally have a higher GDP than those that receive fewer flights, additionally, the flights they do have are generally less full. This is significant because it points towards their being inefficiencies in particular plane flights and which airports these flights typically come from. Furthermore, it gives us an indication that as airports in lower GDP cities have fewer full flights overall, these flights are generally worse for the environment given they are carrying fewer passengers for a given trip compared to a city with a larger GDP leading to the inefficient use of fuel.

Potential future directions for additional work

In the future we can expand upon our analysis by getting additional data and expanding the overall scope of our search. Our current data was slightly mismatched so getting data on GDP from 2000 to 2009 and making use of a greater proportion of our current flight data could give us a better idea of the interaction between city GDP and number and capacity of flight to that city over a larger and more accurate time frame. A further possibility is that using pollution data of the examined cities could allow for seeing if the pollution trends match that of flight inefficiencies.

Conclusion

To conclude our data analysis, we have found that cities with more flights (both inbound and outbound) and higher-density flights in 2009 generally have a higher GDP 8 years later. This is significant because it spots trends in the inefficiencies of plane flights and which airports these flights typically come from. Furthermore, it gives us an indication that although airports in lower GDP cities have less flights overall, their flights are typically worse for the environment since they are less densely packed.
